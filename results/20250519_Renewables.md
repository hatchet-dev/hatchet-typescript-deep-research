## 1. Cost Competitiveness of Renewable Energy Technologies

### Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) and Storage Costs

- **Wind and Solar Power**: According to Lazard's 2024 LCOE+ analysis, large-scale onshore wind power is the cheapest electricity source globally, with LCOE ranging from $27 to $73/MWh and averaging $50/MWh [1][2]. Utility-scale solar PV has an LCOE between $29 and $92/MWh, averaging $61/MWh, which is more than half the cost of coal [1][4]. When combined with energy storage, wind and solar remain competitive, with wind plus storage costing $45-$133/MWh and solar plus storage $60-$210/MWh, both generally cheaper than coal ($69-$168/MWh) and competitive with combined cycle gas ($45-$108/MWh) [1][3][5].

- **Nuclear Power**: Nuclear costs have doubled since 2011, with an average LCOE of $182/MWh in 2024, significantly higher than renewables even with storage [1][6].

- **Energy Storage Systems (ESS)**: Battery storage costs vary by duration; a 100MW four-hour battery costs $170-$296/MWh, cheaper than a one-hour battery at $222-$352/MWh [1][8]. Advances in lithium-ion, pumped hydro, and hydrogen storage technologies are improving storage capacity and efficiency, addressing intermittency challenges [133][140].

### Capital Costs and Capacity Factors

- Capital costs per kW vary widely: onshore wind ($1,462–1,718), solar PV ($1,327–2,743), coal ($3,075–5,542), nuclear ($6,695–7,989) [18][21]. Capacity factors differ by technology, with nuclear up to 94%, geothermal 80–90%, onshore wind 18–48%, and solar PV 12–30% [22].

- Scalability depends on balancing capital costs and capacity factors; nuclear offers high capacity but high capital costs, while wind and solar have lower capital costs but variable output [23].

## 2. Efficiency and Environmental Impact

- **Ground Source Heat Pumps (GSHPs)**: GSHPs are highly energy-efficient HVAC technologies with thermal efficiencies over 300%, reducing energy consumption and emissions by up to 72% compared to electric resistance heating [93][94][96]. However, they have high upfront capital costs (2-5 times conventional systems) but low operational costs and payback periods varying by country [100][103][105].

- Renewable energy technologies produce zero or low emissions locally, but overall environmental impact depends on electricity supply emissions intensity [98].

## 4. Policy Support and Financial Mechanisms

- **Global Investment Trends**: Clean energy investment reached $1.3 trillion globally in 2022, with emerging and developing economies (EMDEs) investing $770 billion, two-thirds from China [108][213].

- **Policy Instruments**: Feed-in tariffs, auctions, renewable portfolio standards, tax credits, subsidies, and concessional finance are widely used to promote renewables [215][224][289][293].

- **Project Preparation and Risk Mitigation**: Standardized frameworks, solar parks (e.g., India’s solar park model), and public-private partnerships reduce risks and attract private capital [148][152][153][155].

- **Challenges in EMDEs**: Higher cost of capital, regulatory uncertainty, grid limitations, and upfront investment barriers require tailored policies and international financial support [114][150][159][169].

- **Sustainable Finance**: ESG factors, green bonds, and climate finance are increasingly important for renewable energy project financing, though regulatory complexity poses challenges [251][257][259].

## 5. Grid Integration and Energy Storage

- Distributed energy resources and decentralized generation (e.g., rooftop solar) enhance resilience and energy access [146][197].
